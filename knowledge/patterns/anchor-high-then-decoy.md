# Anchor high, decoy between anchor and target

## Convergence

Four operators across pricing science, behavioral economics, and services-pricing practice converge on the same three-tier architecture: present the buyer an Anchor (intentionally high; signals what *full scope* costs), a Target (what the operator expects to close), and a Decoy (between anchor and target; makes target feel like the obvious pick). The anchor isn't a price the operator expects to be accepted; it's the reference point the buyer's perception engine binds against. The decoy isn't a real option; it's a structural element that keeps the target from feeling like the floor.

## Operators

- Blair Enns (Win Without Pitching, *Pricing Creativity*), `ins_enns-anchor-high-three-options`. The three-option pricing presentation: present three options where the highest anchors the conversation, the middle is the expected close, the lowest is a deliberate decoy. The rule applies whether the offer is services, products, or hybrid.
- Madhavan Ramanujam (*Monetizing Innovation*), `ins_ramanujam-leaders-fillers-killers`. Bundle composition per tier: Leaders (the must-haves), Fillers (the nice-to-haves), Killers (the dealbreakers the operator removes from low tiers). Each tier carries different combinations; the anchor includes more leaders + fillers; the decoy strips a single high-leverage filler.
- Dan Ariely (*Predictably Irrational*), `ins_ariely-decoy-effect-pricing`. The decoy effect: buyers given two options pick A vs B on absolute merit; buyers given three options pick the middle option ~80% of the time when the third option is structurally inferior on one dimension. The decoy is the asymmetric inferior option that makes the target dominate.
- Hermann Simon (*Confessions of the Pricing Man*), `ins_simon-tier-discipline`. Single-price strategies are always suboptimal because willingness-to-pay is heterogeneous. Multi-tier architectures capture more of the WTP distribution. Tier discipline (price gaps, scope gaps, switching-feature design) is the operator's job, not the buyer's.

## Variation

- Enns operationalises it for *creative services* — three engagement options on every proposal.
- Ramanujam operationalises it for *product tiers* — Leaders/Fillers/Killers bundling per segment.
- Ariely provides the *cognitive grounding* — why the decoy works.
- Simon provides the *organisational discipline* — single-price is always wrong; tier-discipline compounds.
- Convergence: three tiers, not two, not five. Anchor at 2.5-3× the reservation price; target at 1.6-2× reservation; decoy at 2.2-2.5× reservation. Decoy strips one high-leverage element so target dominates on value.

## Implication

For consulting and services operators:

1. **Present three tiers on every proposal.** Anchor / Target / Decoy. Refuse single-tier proposals — they collapse the conversation to a yes/no negotiation on a single number.
2. **Anchor at 2.5-3× the reservation price.** If the anchor is below reservation × 2.5, it doesn't anchor — the decoy collapses and the structure fails. Re-anchor or fall back to single-price (and accept the lower close).
3. **Design the decoy as structurally inferior on one dimension.** Same scope as anchor minus a single high-leverage layer (the customer-call quota, the calibration tier, the eval-rubric production). The decoy must be visibly *worse than anchor* on one axis so target is the dominated middle.
4. **Name the switching feature.** What deliverable moves the prospect from target to anchor? Calibration ledger? Substrate handover? Customer-call corpus depth? Name it on the proposal so the prospect understands why anchor exists.
5. **Hold the line on the floor.** Reservation price is non-negotiable. Discounting collapses the architecture (Simon) and trains future prospects to anchor low.

## Counter-evidence

- For **commodity-priced services** (compliance audits, SOC 2 packages, fixed-deliverable graphic design at scale), the buyer arrives with an external reference price. Behavioral architecture is muted; price-shopping dominates. Three-tier still applies but the spread compresses.
- For **enterprise procurement** with formal RFP processes, the three-tier presentation may be filtered out of the buying flow entirely — procurement teams force a single bid against a spec. Operators bid the target as a single number and reserve the anchor/decoy structure for the founder/CMO conversation that precedes RFP.
- For **time-and-materials engagements** with no fixed scope, the three-tier structure inverts to *anchor / target / decoy on hourly rate × estimated hours range*. Ramanujam and Stark both flag this as inferior to fixed-fee productized work.
