# Paid-social creative fatigues on a measurable window, refresh cadence is the lever

## Convergence

Four operators with deep paid-social practice converge on the same operating answer: on Meta, TikTok, and the broader social-feed surface, creative is the variable that drives performance, not bidding, audience tuning, or budget allocation. Creatives lose efficiency on a measurable window (commonly weeks 3 through 5 for a single asset against a single audience), and refresh cadence is the practitioner's most leveraged lever. Trying to fix a fatiguing campaign through bidding or audience expansion treats the symptom; producing a new variant treats the cause.

## Operators

- **Susan Wenograd**, paid-social practitioner and former Aimclear / 9Sail leader. Public posts and conference talks consistently argue that "creative is the targeting" on social platforms: the algorithm's job is to find people who respond to a given creative, not the other way around. When a creative tires, the audience hasn't disappeared, the message has stopped landing.
- **Andrew Foxwell**, e-commerce paid-social agency owner (Foxwell Digital), co-host of the *E-commerce Influence* podcast. Foxwell's repeated public position is that creative volume is the lever in 2025 and beyond: brands shipping 10+ creatives per week against a brief outperform brands shipping two, even when the average quality is similar, because the second-best creative becomes the next workhorse when the leader fades.
- **Ralph Burns**, founder of Tier 11 (formerly Dominate Web Media), host of the *Perpetual Traffic* podcast. Burns has long argued for an asset-led structure (hook, story, offer) and a deliberate refresh cadence over bid-led optimisation. His operating frame, repeated across podcast episodes and case studies, is that paid-social CAC is upstream of creative fatigue, not bidding.
- **Aaron Orendorff**, former editor-in-chief of Shopify Plus, content director at Common Thread Collective. Orendorff's writing on paid social, especially the *Common Thread Collective* analyses, frames the creative-fatigue cycle as the actual unit of paid-ads operations: the ratio of new creative tested per week against the budget weight a campaign carries.

## Variation

- **Wenograd** names the *attribution shift*: creative is the targeting; audience tuning is post-creative.
- **Foxwell** names the *volume requirement*: ship 10+ per week, accept that 70-80% will not become workhorses.
- **Burns** names the *structural alternative*: bidding is downstream; asset structure (hook, story, offer) and refresh cadence are upstream.
- **Orendorff** names the *operating cycle*: weekly cadence on creative production is the unit of paid-ads operations.
- Convergence: a paid-social account run without a refresh cadence is allocating budget to fatiguing assets. CAC drift is the visible symptom; missing creative pipeline is the actual cause.

## Implication

For paid-social and broader social-feed paid placements:

1. **Stand up a refresh cadence as a first-class routine**, not as a reaction to dropping CTR. Default cadence: 2-4 new variants per week per active campaign for retail / e-commerce; 1-2 per week for B2B SaaS where creative production is more expensive.
2. **Define fatigue thresholds before any campaign opens.** Defaults that stay out of the worst trouble: 30% CTR decline over 14 days from peak, frequency above 3.0 on Meta, CPM rise above 25%. When any threshold trips, the variant is queued for refresh, not for bid adjustment.
3. **Track production volume, not just performance**, in the paid-ads dashboard. Weeks where new-creative volume falls below the cadence target are leading indicators of CAC drift 14-21 days later.
4. **Bidding and audience changes go second.** When a creative is fatiguing, the cheap intervention is a new creative; bid changes mostly buy time and obscure the underlying production gap.

## Counter-evidence

- **Search ads on Google** do not exhibit the same fatigue curve, because the buyer triggers the impression. Search ads can run for months on the same headline and description against fresh queries; treating search creative the same way wastes production capacity.
- **Some B2B brand campaigns** running against a small, slow-moving audience may see slower fatigue (8-12 weeks) because the audience refreshes more slowly than the creative tires. The cadence still applies; the multiplier is smaller.
- **Performance Max and similar AI-rotation surfaces** introduce a layer between creative and impression that makes single-creative fatigue harder to measure. The pattern still applies at the asset-group level; the diagnostic shifts.
