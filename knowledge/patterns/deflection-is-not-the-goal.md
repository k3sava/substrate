# Deflection is not the goal; right-routing is the goal

## Convergence

Four operators with different vantage points (loyalty research originator, customer-experience consulting, subscription-economy thought leadership, customer-success operator practice) converge on the same operating claim: optimizing for ticket deflection as a primary metric corrupts the support function and produces measurably worse retention. The shape they reject is the deflection-cost-savings trap: chatbot deflects N% of tickets, headcount or ticket-handling cost drops M dollars, the metric is reported as a win, and the underlying customer experience deteriorates as customers route around or escape from the bot to find a human.

The shape they converge on is right-routing: every customer with a question gets routed to the cheapest channel that *actually answers it*. Self-serve content for the answerable; community for the experience-grounded; chatbot for the simple-and-known; human for the complex, the urgent, or the upset. The metric of the system is not "tickets deflected," it is "customer got to the right answer in the right window." Deflection is a side effect of right-routing done well, not the goal of the system.

## Operators

- **Frederick Reichheld**, Bain & Company, NPS originator. The Loyalty Effect (1996) and Effort Score research argue that customer effort to resolve a problem is the strongest predictor of loyalty erosion. A bot that deflects a ticket but leaves the customer with a partial answer raises effort, not lowers it. Reichheld's framing: the goal is to reduce effort to resolution, which sometimes means *more* human contact, not less. Deflection-as-goal inverts the relationship and produces NPS decay.
- **Bain CX research, customer effort score (CES) literature**. Bain's customer-effort research (built on the Reichheld framework and extended through subsequent enterprise studies) consistently finds that channel-forcing (making customers use a chatbot when they need a human) is a net-negative loyalty driver, while right-routing (cheap channel for cheap question, expensive channel for expensive question) is a net-positive loyalty driver. The economics of right-routing usually beat the economics of deflection-maximization once the loyalty math is included.
- **Tien Tzuo**, Zuora founder, "Subscribed" author. The subscription-economy frame on support: in a subscription business, every customer interaction is a renewal moment. Reframing support as "the cheapest renewal call you'll ever run" exposes the deflection-as-goal failure: a deflected ticket from an unhappy customer is a deferred churn signal, not a saved cost. Tzuo's recommendation: measure support quality on retention impact, not on deflection rate.
- **Lincoln Murphy**, customer success operator. Murphy's customer-success-as-revenue-protection lens treats every support contact as a leading indicator. Deflection-maximization removes the leading indicator without addressing the underlying friction. Murphy's reframe: the goal is to make the *right* support contacts cheaper to resolve, not to make support contact harder to initiate. The customer who needed help and could not find it is the customer who churns.

## Variation

- Reichheld frames it through *effort and loyalty math* (deflection raises effort, lowers loyalty).
- Bain CX research frames it through *empirical loyalty curves* (channel-forcing is net-negative).
- Tzuo frames it through *subscription-renewal math* (every contact is a renewal moment).
- Murphy frames it through *customer-success operator practice* (deflection removes the leading indicator).
- Convergence: optimizing for deflection-as-goal degrades retention; optimizing for right-routing improves retention; the right metric is "customer got the right answer in the right window," not "tickets deflected."

## Implication

Support-program design must be measured on right-routing accuracy, not deflection rate. The deflection number is acceptable as a side metric, never as a primary metric. Skills that design support automation must specify the routing logic per intent type and must measure the loyalty / NPS / retention impact alongside the cost-savings impact. A program that lowers tickets-per-account by 30% but lowers CSAT by 10pp is not a win; it is an unrealized churn cost showing up two quarters later.

The right-routing frame produces a four-tier ladder: self-serve content (article, video, walkthrough) for the questions a documented answer addresses; community for the experience-grounded questions where a peer's answer beats a vendor's; chatbot or in-product agent for the simple-and-known where the routing logic is reliable; human for the complex, the urgent, the upset, or anything the prior tiers cannot resolve. The system is well-designed when each tier handles the questions it should and escalates the rest cleanly. The system is mis-designed when any tier holds onto a question it cannot resolve to drive deflection numbers.

Skills that touch support automation must declare the routing logic, not just the deflection target. Skills that design help content must read both the tickets the content addresses and the tickets the content fails to address (the escalation rate is the truth). Refusal: any program that proposes deflection rate as the primary KPI must justify why that is not the right-routing failure mode this pattern names.
