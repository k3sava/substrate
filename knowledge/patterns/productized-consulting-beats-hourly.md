# Productized fixed-fee consulting beats hourly billing

## Convergence

Four operators across services-pricing practice converge on the same operator-economics claim: hourly billing is the consulting trap. It punishes efficiency (faster delivery = lower fee), invites scope-slide ("just one more thing"), and signals commodity (the client buys hours, not outcomes). Productized fixed-fee engagements with named scope, named price, named refusal-criterion produce better operator margin AND better client trust. The four cite the same root cause: hourly billing prices the *input* (operator time) when the client buys the *output* (a substrate, an artifact, a calibrated bet).

## Operators

- Jonathan Stark, `ins_stark-hourly-billing-is-nuts`. Book of the same name. The core argument: hourly billing creates incentive misalignment in every direction — operator slows down, client suspects every invoice, scope drifts because there's no scope contract. Replace with fixed-fee productized offers tied to outcomes.
- Blair Enns (Win Without Pitching, *Pricing Creativity*), `ins_enns-value-priced-engagements`. Value-pricing methodology: price the engagement against the *client outcome*, not the operator time. Three-option presentation (see `pat_anchor-high-then-decoy`) operates on top of fixed-fee architecture, not hourly.
- David C. Baker (*The Business of Expertise*), `ins_baker-positioning-sets-price-floor`. Positioning sets the price floor; productization sets the operator economics. A specialised operator with a clear positioning commands a fixed-fee premium; a generalist on hourly is commoditised by definition.
- Alan Weiss (*Million-Dollar Consulting*), `ins_weiss-value-based-fees`. Value-based fees: the engagement price is a function of the value created for the client, not the operator's hours. Weiss's rule: never quote time; always quote outcome. Hourly billing is the floor of the consulting market; value-pricing is the ceiling.

## Variation

- Stark provides the *operator-economics frame* — hourly billing punishes the efficient operator.
- Enns operationalises it as a *proposal architecture* — three options, all fixed-fee, none hourly.
- Baker provides the *positioning-pricing link* — positioning earns the right to fixed-fee.
- Weiss provides the *value-pricing methodology* — price as function of client outcome.
- Convergence: fixed-fee + productized scope + outcome-grounded price is the operator-side substrate. Hourly billing is the failure mode the four operators agree on.

## Implication

For independent operators, fractional PMMs, agency leads:

1. **Refuse hourly engagements as the default.** Hourly is the trap, not the offer. If a client insists on hourly, the engagement is mis-positioned upstream — the operator hasn't earned the fixed-fee ask.
2. **Productize every engagement shape.** Phase-0 audit, 5-day POC, 4-8-week embedded, 3-month retainer, 2-week train-team — each has named scope, named deliverables, named price.
3. **Refuse "more hours" as the switching feature.** The switching feature between tiers must be a deliverable (the calibration ledger, the substrate handover, the customer-call corpus depth) — never additional hours. Hours-based scope-flex is the trap repackaged.
4. **Tie the price to the outcome substrate.** A 3-month retainer price isn't "12 weeks of operator time" — it's "the substrate the team owns after, plus three calibrated bets opened and scored." Weiss / Baker both flag this.
5. **Hold the line on discount.** Discounting fixed-fee engagements collapses the productization (Simon, Enns). If the client can't pay the price, the engagement is mis-shaped, not mis-priced.

## Counter-evidence

- For **deep-uncertainty exploratory engagements** (a true diagnostic on a problem the operator has never seen, no calibration history, no productized scope) hourly may be the honest pricing model — for a defined research window only. Stark accepts this exception explicitly: "hourly is OK for *discovery*; never for *delivery*."
- For **agency-internal work** (an agency lead managing a team of operators), hourly is the internal cost-accounting model even when the external client price is fixed-fee. The operator-facing rule still applies; the agency-facing rule is a different layer.
- For **regulated services** (legal, accounting, compliance audit) where hourly billing is the industry default and the client expects it as the trust signal, productization is still possible — bundled "package" pricing on top of hourly — but the friction is higher.
