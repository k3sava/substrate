# Strategic narrative is a five-act movie structure

## Convergence

Four operators across narrative consulting (Raskin), positioning (Dunford), homepage compression (Pierri), and category design (Lochhead) converge on the same architecture for a strategic narrative: it is a five-act story that names a change in the world, raises the stakes, promises a "promised land," names the obstacles, and pitches the resolution. The story is the strategy expressed in compressed form. Decks, homepages, ad copy, and sales pitches are downstream renderings. If the five acts don't hold together, no amount of design polish recovers the asset.

## The five acts

1. **Name a change in the world.** Not a product change. A market change, a buyer change, a technology change, a regulatory change. Specific and undeniable.
2. **Raise the stakes.** What happens to the buyer who does not adapt to the change? Loss aversion is the engine; the cost of inaction must clear the 2× tax.
3. **Promise the "promised land."** Describe the post-resolution state in the buyer's vocabulary, not the marketer's. Raskin: "the happy ending of your customer's story."
4. **Name the obstacles.** What stands between the buyer and the promised land? Frame these as obstacles to a *new* game, not problems on the current one. Features become gates the buyer must pass to win the new game.
5. **Pitch the resolution.** Only here does the product appear. The product is the magic the protagonist (the buyer) needs to overcome the obstacles. Not the hero. The gift the hero receives from the guide.

## Operators

- **Andy Raskin**, `ins_company-story-is-the-strategy`, `ins_old-game-to-new-game-narrative`, `ins_obstacles-as-gates-not-problems`. The five-act architecture is Raskin's full frame; he distills it from movie structure (Save the Cat, Hero's Journey) and applies it to B2B strategic narrative.
- **April Dunford**, `ins_setup-follow-through-pitch`, `ins_dunford-positioning-is-context`. Sales pitches need a Setup before the Follow-Through; the Setup runs Acts 1-2 (change + stakes) before any product mention. Positioning is the context the prospect needs to value the product.
- **Anthony Pierri**, `ins_homepage-five-second-trinity`, `ins_pierri-one-buyer-one-message`. The homepage is Acts 1 and 5 compressed into 5 seconds: name the change (or the use case the change creates), then name the resolution (the product as the magic). One buyer, one message; the five-act discipline forces the choice.
- **Christopher Lochhead** (category design playbook). Category design is the act of *naming the change* publicly enough that the market reorganizes around it. Without Act 1, every other act is selling into someone else's category.

## Variation

- **Raskin** owns the *full architecture*, the canonical five-act frame.
- **Dunford** owns the *sales-pitch implementation*, where Acts 1-2 are the Setup, Acts 3-5 are the Follow-Through.
- **Pierri** owns the *homepage compression*, where Acts 1 and 5 carry the five-second test.
- **Lochhead** owns the *category-design implementation*, where Act 1 is the public act of naming the change so the category exists.
- Convergence: same five-act movie structure, different surfaces of execution.

## Implication

For PMMs, founders, and content leaders shipping strategic narrative:

1. **Write the narrative as five acts before any artifact.** The deck, the homepage, the ad, the sales pitch, the launch post, all are renderings of the same five-act story. If the story doesn't work, no surface does.
2. **Audit each surface against the five acts.** Run a monthly narrative-drift audit (Raskin). Does the homepage name the change? Does the deck raise the stakes before the product appears? Does the sales pitch run Setup-before-Follow-Through? If any surface skips an act, fix it before launching anything new.
3. **Name the change in 1-3 words.** Compression is the test. A change that takes a paragraph to name is not yet a strategic narrative; it is still research.
4. **Treat the product as the gift, not the hero.** Per StoryBrand and Raskin both: the customer is the hero, the brand is the guide, the product is the magic. Reverse this and the narrative collapses into feature-marketing.
5. **Cite buyer language in Acts 2 and 3.** The stakes (Act 2) and the promised land (Act 3) must use verbatim language from VoC. Marketer-language in these acts is the load-bearing failure mode that produces flat narrative.

## Counter-evidence

- **Greenfield categories** with no incumbent collapse Act 1 (no change in the world to name; the category itself is the change). The five-act frame still applies but Act 1 becomes the category-definition move.
- **Forced-buy moments** (compliance migration, billing crisis, regulatory shift) collapse Acts 1-2; the buyer already feels the stakes. Skip directly to Acts 3-5.
- **PLG bottom-up adoption** with frictionless onboarding sometimes carries the narrative through product UX rather than copy; the five acts still exist but are expressed as in-product moments rather than copy beats.
