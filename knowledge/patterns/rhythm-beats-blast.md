# Consistent rhythm beats episodic blast

## Convergence

Four operators across prospecting discipline (Jeb Blount), multichannel sales coaching (John Barrows), inside-sales-org design (Trish Bertuzzi), and personalization-at-scale (Becc Holland) converge on the same operating claim: a consistent 30-60-90 day cadence with distinct content gates and channel rotation outperforms episodic burst-and-quiet patterns. The burst pattern (10 emails in two days, then nothing for three weeks) reads as desperation when the buyer is in-window and as absence when the buyer is out-of-window. The rhythm pattern (8-12 touches across 30 days, mixed channels, distinct content per touch) maintains presence without saturation, and crucially, every touch has a reason to exist beyond the cadence schedule.

## Operators

- **Jeb Blount** (Sales Gravy, Fanatical Prospecting), `ins_blount-30-day-cadence-discipline`. Blount's "30-day rule": consistent prospecting effort over 30+ days separates top-quartile sellers from the bottom three quartiles. The bottom-quartile pattern is binge-and-purge, "I'll prospect heavily this week, then focus on closing the deals it produces." The top-quartile pattern is steady daily volume with monthly review of which accounts moved. Blount's data: top quartile sellers do 3-4x the dial volume of bottom quartile, but the volume gap is the rhythm, not the intensity.
- **John Barrows** (JB Sales), `ins_barrows-multichannel-touch-pattern`. Barrows's cadence design: each touch must use a different channel (email, LinkedIn, voicemail, video, SMS, referral) and each touch must add new information (new content piece, new question, new artifact, new POV) — not "just checking in." A touch that says "did you see my last email?" is wasted because it triggers the same brain pattern as the previous touch. Channel rotation + content rotation breaks the pattern-recognition the buyer's spam filter (mental and literal) is using.
- **Trish Bertuzzi** (The Bridge Group), `ins_bertuzzi-pursuit-not-chase`. Bertuzzi's framing: pursuit (consistent rhythm + value-add per touch) compounds; chase (frantic outreach when pipeline is dry, then radio silence when pipeline is full) erodes pipeline quality and brand. Bertuzzi's research on inside-sales orgs: orgs running 15+ touch sequences with rhythm see 2.5x meeting rates over orgs running 8-touch burst sequences, holding industry and ACV constant.
- **Becc Holland** (Flip the Script), `ins_holland-cadence-content-gates`. Holland's content gates inside cadence: each touch hits a distinct content gate, problem-frame → cost-of-inaction → proof point → differentiator → executive-level reframe → final ask. Touches that land on the same gate twice (e.g., two "problem frames" in a row) waste a slot. The gate progression mirrors the buyer's stages of awareness and forces the seller to actually move the conversation.

## Variation

- **Blount** names the *time horizon*: 30 days minimum to detect rhythm signal vs. burst noise.
- **Barrows** names the *channel + content rotation rule*: every touch is a new channel + new information.
- **Bertuzzi** names the *org-level effect*: rhythm orgs outperform burst orgs at the org level, not just the rep level.
- **Holland** names the *content-gate progression*: each touch advances the buyer's awareness stage.

A complete cadence-design practice uses all four: 30+ day windows (Blount), channel + content rotation per touch (Barrows), org-level rhythm discipline (Bertuzzi), and distinct content gates per touch (Holland).

## Implication

For SDR managers, AEs running their own pipeline, and ABM teams:

1. **Design the cadence as a series of distinct moments.** Each touch has a content type (problem-frame, proof, differentiator, executive-reframe, ask) that no prior touch has hit.
2. **Rotate channels deliberately.** The default 7-touch all-email sequence is a pattern the buyer's filter recognizes. Email + LinkedIn + voice + video + SMS + referral spreads the signal across channels the filter doesn't co-correlate.
3. **Run the cadence over 30+ days, not 7-10.** Burst sequences harvest the lowest-friction segment and burn the rest. Rhythm sequences hold presence long enough for the buyer's "why now" to develop.
4. **Audit the cadence for "just checking in" touches.** These are filler. Replace with a content gate that hasn't been hit yet (or end the cadence).
5. **Measure cadence at the account level for trigger-event ABM.** Inside a fresh trigger window, cadence tightens (8-12 touches in 30 days). Outside the window, cadence loosens (3-4 touches per quarter).

## Counter-evidence

- **High-velocity transactional sales** (sub-$10K, 7-day close) often work best with a 5-7 touch sprint cadence; 30 days is longer than the deal cycle.
- **Compliance-mandate buys** have their own timer; cadence rhythm is irrelevant once the deadline forces the action.
- **Account list churn faster than 30 days** breaks rhythm; the list refreshes before the rhythm produces signal.
- **Pure inbound** doesn't need cadence design; the buyer is already in motion. Cadence design matters for outbound and ABM.
