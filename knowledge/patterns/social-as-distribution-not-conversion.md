# Social drives top-of-funnel awareness, last-click attribution underweights its real role

## Convergence

Three operators with deep growth-marketing practice converge on the same operating distinction: social is fundamentally a top-of-funnel surface that compounds awareness, audience, and brand recall, not a direct-response surface that closes deals. The last-click attribution model that most analytics tools default to undercounts social's contribution by a structural margin (commonly 50 to 80 percent of social's true effect goes uncredited), because the buyer who saw the social post on Tuesday and Googled the company on Friday gets credited to organic search, not to social. A growth program that allocates social budget on last-click CAC will starve the channel that is feeding the brand-search and direct-traffic numbers it credits to other channels.

## Operators

- **Andrew Chen**, partner at Andreessen Horowitz, formerly head of Rider Growth at Uber. Chen's writing on the *paid-acquisition ceiling* (the recurring observation that paid-channel CAC inflates over time as the channel saturates) consistently names earned and organic-social as the cohort-warming surface that holds CAC down across the program. His framing: paid is the loop that turns awareness into pipeline; social is the surface that builds the awareness paid converts. Treating them as separable channels with their own CAC numbers is the misallocation pattern.
- **Lenny Rachitsky**, formerly Airbnb product lead, now publisher of *Lenny's Newsletter* with a documented growth path from social to email to paid product. Rachitsky has written and discussed at length the *content distribution flywheel* model: social posts drive newsletter subscriptions; newsletter compounds into product trial; trial converts to revenue. Each step is measurable, but the social-to-newsletter step rarely shows up in CAC dashboards because newsletter is treated as "owned" rather than "social-attributed." The flywheel is one mechanism; cutting any stage breaks the others.
- **Joel Klettke**, copywriter and conversion strategist (Business Casual Copywriting). Klettke's repeated public position is that social copy and landing page copy do different jobs: social copy warms; landing page copy converts. Treating social like a landing page (direct calls, conversion-language) underperforms because the reader is not in conversion mindset. Treating the landing page like a social post (open-ended hook, conversational warmth) underperforms because the reader is in conversion mindset. The two surfaces require different copy disciplines because they sit at different funnel stages.

## Variation

- **Chen** names the *budget-allocation level*: paid is the converter, social is the awareness surface; allocating on last-click starves the awareness surface.
- **Rachitsky** names the *content-flywheel level*: social is the on-ramp, newsletter is the warm middle, product is the close. Each stage has its own metric; cross-stage attribution is fragile.
- **Klettke** names the *copy-discipline level*: social copy and conversion copy do different jobs; the disciplines are not interchangeable.
- Convergence: social is not a direct-response channel and should not be measured like one. The fix is to track cohort-level metrics (audience overlap with closed-won, branded-search lift, direct-traffic lift, newsletter-from-social conversion) rather than last-click CAC.

## Implication

For social-media programs and the leadership that funds them:

1. **Stop measuring social on last-click CAC.** The number is structurally undercounting; using it as the funding signal will starve a channel that is doing real work. Replace it with cohort-level metrics: branded-search lift in the 30-90 days after a campaign launch, direct-traffic lift, audience overlap with closed-won (LinkedIn audience-overlap reporting is the closest tool), newsletter or trial signups attributed via UTM where social is a primary touchpoint.
2. **Define social's job in the funnel before defining its KPI.** Social drives one or more of: brand awareness, audience growth, demand shaping, sales-conversation enablement, recruiter pipeline, customer loyalty. Each job has its own metric. A program that says "social drives leads" without picking which job is the primary one will end up measured against last-click and starved.
3. **Build a social-to-warm-channel handoff explicitly.** The reader who reads a LinkedIn post is two steps from a sales conversation, not zero. The intermediate step (newsletter signup, free resource download, product-led tour, search-and-click) is where the conversion is logged. Track the handoff as a named conversion event; do not let the analytics tool default it to the search or direct channel.
4. **Use incrementality testing for big social investments.** When the program scales, an incrementality test (region holdout, time-based holdout) is the only honest way to size social's contribution. Run one when the channel exceeds 5 percent of marketing spend or when leadership questions the budget.
5. **Match copy discipline to funnel stage.** Social posts open with hooks, ask questions, build narrative; landing pages and ads close with calls, anchor on outcomes, present specific proof. A program that confuses the two underperforms on both surfaces.

## Counter-evidence

- **Direct-response social campaigns on Meta and TikTok** can drive measurable last-click conversions when the offer, audience, and creative align. This is a special case (typically e-commerce, app install, lead-gen with a high-intent cohort) where social's awareness role is bypassed by paid intent capture. The pattern still applies for organic social and for B2B paid social with longer cycles.
- **Some category-creating B2B brands** find that social is the only viable demand-generation channel for years and run it on direct-response logic out of necessity. Even here, the cohort metric (eventually-converted audience members from social) is more honest than the last-click metric, and the pattern's prescription holds: track cohort, not click.
- **Mature category brands** with consistent search demand may find that social's role narrows to retention and loyalty (existing customers reinforced rather than new prospects attracted). This is a healthy specialisation, not a refutation. The pattern still names the job correctly: distribution and awareness, not last-click conversion.
