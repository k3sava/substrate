# Tickets are the highest-fidelity product feedback channel; treat them as product knowledge

## Convergence

Four operators with very different lenses (CEO of a workplace tools company, founder of a programmatic-display giant, retention-research founder, prolific operator/investor) converge on the same structural claim: support tickets are not a cost center to be minimized, they are the highest-fidelity, lowest-cost feedback channel a product team has access to. Customers who file tickets are spending their time and goodwill to tell the company what is broken, what is confusing, and what is missing. The volume, the topical clusters, and the verbatim language inside tickets surface product, positioning, pricing, and onboarding gaps that survey research and analytics dashboards mute or miss entirely.

The shape repeats: read the tickets verbatim, cluster them by theme, count the cluster sizes, sample the language, feed the clusters into the product roadmap, the help docs catalog, the onboarding flow, and the positioning brief. The teams that do this compound on every conversation. The teams that route tickets to a support queue and let them die compound nothing.

## Operators

- **Stewart Butterfield**, co-founder Slack, ex-Flickr. Slack ran a "support is product" doctrine in the early years: every ticket got read by a product person, ticket clusters seeded the roadmap, and the support team's qualitative reads were treated as load-bearing evidence in product decisions. Butterfield was explicit that support volume was a feedback channel, not a problem to deflect. The early Slack onboarding flow was rebuilt three times based on ticket clusters around team setup and channel discovery.
- **Tom Klein**, ex-MindShare programmatic operator. The "support reads like a product brief" frame: tickets are a product brief, written daily by users, addressed to whoever bothers to read them. Klein argues tickets surface the highest-leverage product changes because they map exactly to friction the user experienced in the last 24 hours, not to friction the user remembers and self-reports a quarter later in a survey.
- **Sahil Bloom**, multi-company operator and writer on compounding. The compounding-knowledge framing of tickets: each ticket is a unit of customer language and friction. Reading 50 tickets a week compounds into a fluency in customer language that no quarterly survey produces. Teams that read tickets weekly write better copy, land better positioning, and ship better onboarding because the language and the friction are present in their head.
- **Patrick Campbell**, ProfitWell / Paddle. The retention-research finding that support volume per account is one of the strongest leading indicators of churn (30-90 days ahead of cancellation), and that the *content* of the support tickets is the cohort-level diagnostic for retention. Campbell's argument: a churn-prediction model that ignores support content is operating on partial substrate; a retention program that does not feed support clusters into product is missing the lever.

## Variation

- Butterfield frames it as *operating doctrine* (support is product, every ticket gets read).
- Klein frames it as *daily product brief* (tickets are the brief, written by users).
- Bloom frames it as *compounding fluency* (reading tickets compounds customer-language fluency).
- Campbell frames it as *retention substrate* (ticket content is the cohort-level diagnostic).
- Convergence: tickets are first-class product feedback. Treat them as product knowledge, cluster them, feed them upstream, and compound on every conversation.

## Implication

Tickets must be read, not just resolved. Treat the ticket corpus as a product-knowledge artifact with the same standing as the product manifest, the ICP, and the positioning brief. Cluster tickets by theme on a weekly or monthly cadence. Feed cluster sizes and verbatim language into the product roadmap, the help docs catalog, the onboarding flow, the positioning brief, and the buyer-facing copy.

Skills that touch support data must read tickets verbatim before generating recommendations. Skills that produce help content must trace each article back to a ticket cluster of at least 5 sources, in customer language. Skills that diagnose churn must read support volume and content as a primary feature, not an afterthought.

The reverse failure mode is treating tickets as a support-team-only artifact: tickets get categorized for routing, response-time SLA gets tracked, deflection rate gets reported, and the *content* of the tickets never crosses the support team boundary. This is the most common failure pattern and the one this pattern exists to prevent.
