# Trigger events outperform blanket cadence

## Convergence

Four operators across cold-outbound architecture (Aaron Ross), prospecting psychology (Jeb Blount), multithread account pursuit (Sam Nelson), and personalization-at-scale (Becc Holland) converge on the same operating claim: outbound aimed at companies inside a *trigger-event window* (funding round, exec hire, product launch, layoff, acquisition, tech-stack shift) outperforms blanket cadence at every stage of the funnel. The relevance signal does the heavy lifting; the cadence carries it. Reverse the order and the cadence is doing the work alone, which is why blanket sequences hover near 1% reply rates while trigger-driven sequences clear 8-12%.

## Operators

- **Aaron Ross** (Predictable Revenue), `ins_ross-trigger-event-outbound`. Ross's "Cold Calling 2.0" architecture explicitly separates trigger-driven outbound from blanket prospecting. The trigger gives the SDR a non-creepy reason for the call, "I saw you just hired a new VP of CX, here's what most teams do at that stage." Ross's claim: trigger-anchored sequences beat random list-pull sequences by 5-10x on connect-and-convert rates inside the first 90 days of the trigger.
- **Jeb Blount** (Fanatical Prospecting), `ins_blount-prospecting-relevance-window`. Blount's discipline: every outbound touch must answer "why now." Without a "why now" anchor, the touch is interchangeable with the 200 other emails in the buyer's inbox that week. Trigger events provide the cleanest "why now": the buyer's situation just changed, the question is real, the inertia is at its lowest.
- **Sam Nelson** (Outreach, Sales Assembly), `ins_nelson-multithread-on-trigger`. Nelson's account-pursuit model: trigger events are the moment to multithread. A funding round triggers outreach to CFO, COO, and CRO simultaneously, not just the original VP-Sales contact. Why: the trigger creates a 60-90 day window when the budget is being re-allocated and new initiatives are under active discussion. Outside the window, you're cold; inside it, you're timely.
- **Becc Holland** (Flip the Script), `ins_holland-personalization-from-event-not-bio`. Holland's core teaching: personalization grounded in a recent *event* (10-K language, earnings-call quote, exec hire announcement, blog post, podcast appearance) outperforms personalization grounded in *bio facts* (school, prior employer, hometown) by 3-5x reply rates. Bio facts are stale. Events are fresh. The freshness signals the seller did real work, not just LinkedIn-stalk.

## Variation

- **Ross** names the *architectural separation*: trigger-driven outbound is its own motion with its own metrics, not a sub-segment of generic outbound.
- **Blount** names the *psychological anchor*: every touch must justify why this conversation matters today.
- **Nelson** names the *account-level response*: triggers open a multi-buyer window, not a single-contact moment.
- **Holland** names the *content shape*: personalization rooted in events beats personalization rooted in bios.

A complete trigger-event practice uses all four: detect the trigger (Ross's architecture), use it as the "why now" anchor (Blount's discipline), multithread inside the window (Nelson's model), with content that cites the specific event (Holland's craft).

## Implication

For SDR teams, AEs, and ABM operators:

1. **Build trigger detection as a first-class system.** RSS, news APIs, exec-hire trackers, funding databases, layoff trackers, product-launch monitors. Every target account has a trigger feed.
2. **Define the trigger-event windows.** Funding 60 days, exec hire 90 days, product launch 30 days, acquisition 120 days. Outside the window, the trigger is stale.
3. **Cohort accounts by trigger state, not by tier alone.** A Tier 1B account inside a fresh funding window outranks a Tier 1A account with no recent trigger.
4. **Multithread on trigger, single-thread on baseline.** Triggers earn the right to reach 3-5 buyers; without a trigger, single-thread to the most likely owner.
5. **Personalize from the event, not the bio.** First-line opener references the specific trigger artifact: the 10-K passage, the funding-round press release, the exec announcement. No bio openers.

## Counter-evidence

- **Pure inbound / PLG motions** don't need this; the buyer's self-served signal is the trigger.
- **High-velocity transactional sales** (sub-$10K ACV, 7-day close) often can't afford the per-account research required to detect and use triggers; volume cadence wins.
- **Compliance-driven mandatory buys** (SOC2 deadline, regulatory migration) have their own urgency; a trigger frame is redundant and can feel manipulative.
- **Account-list churn**: if the target account list refreshes faster than 30 days, trigger detection lags and the system underperforms a properly-sequenced blanket motion.
