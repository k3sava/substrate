# How we write

A short rulebook. Every external draft passes the voice check before it ships. The check is a regex sweep plus a sentence-length analyzer plus a kill-list match.

## Cadence

Sentence length variance per Gary Provost:

> "This sentence has five words. Here are five more words. Five-word sentences are fine. But several together become monotonous. Listen to what is happening. The writing is getting boring. The sound of it drones. It's like a stuck record. The ear demands some variety."

Mix short, medium, and longer sentences. A draft of all 10-word sentences reads like a press release nobody asked for. A draft of all 30-word sentences reads like a legal document. Vary length on purpose.

## No em dashes in body

Em dashes are an AI-fingerprint signal in 2026. Use commas, periods, parentheses, or colons instead. Headlines and pull quotes can take em dashes. Body copy can't.

## No throat-clearing openers

Banned starts:

- "In today's fast-paced world..."
- "It's no secret that..."
- "We all know that..."
- "Have you ever wondered..."
- "Picture this:"
- "Imagine a world where..."

Open with the claim, not the warm-up. The reader hired the draft to make a point; make it.

## Kill-list

Each project has its own kill-list — the phrases banned in that project's copy. Common starters across many projects:

- orchestrate / orchestration
- seamless / seamlessly
- bespoke
- leverage (as a verb)
- transform (as a generic verb)
- holistic
- synergy / synergize
- unlock (as a generic verb)
- robust (as filler)
- cutting-edge
- best-in-class
- world-class
- next-generation
- AI-powered (without specifics)

The list grows when a phrase shows up in a draft and the project decides "no more of that." It rarely shrinks.

## Outcome-led, not feature-led

Open with what the reader gets. Features come after. "Ship invoices in 90 seconds" before "Our invoice generator." The kill-list pattern can't catch this; the cadence rule can't catch it; this is a content rule the operator has to apply.

## Active voice, default

Passive voice has its uses. They're rarer than people think. "The button was clicked by the user" is a sentence written by a system, not a person. "The user clicked the button" is what the writer meant. Default active.

## Specific over generic

"Sales teams" is generic. "Outbound SDR teams running 5-50 reps" is specific. The specific version reads as if you know who you're writing for. The generic version reads as if you don't.

When the draft has to use a generic noun (because the audience is broad), put the specific noun in the next sentence. "GTM teams know the routine. SDRs cold-call; AEs run discovery; CS hands off the close. ..."

## Editing pattern

Three passes:

1. Cut every sentence that doesn't move the argument.
2. Replace every generic noun with a specific one where the audience supports it.
3. Read the draft aloud. Sentences that sound robotic when you hear them are robotic when read.

Most drafts get tighter on the first pass and more specific on the second. The third pass catches what the gates can't — the rhythm.

## What's in scope for this rule

Customer-facing prose: landing pages, ads, emails, content articles, help docs, video scripts, sales decks visible to buyers.

Out of scope: internal docs (this file is internal; it doesn't run through the gate), code, data, SQL, frontmatter.
