# Q3 launch — diagnosis before plan

The exec ask was "write me a launch plan." This brief refuses the playbook ask and runs a diagnosis first, sourced from frontline contact (sales calls + JTBD switch interviews), then offers three divergent paths instead of a single deliverable. Source content for every claim is a logged conversation; nothing here came from desk research.

## Diagnosis

Two pipeline-loss reasons dominate, surfaced from this quarter's frontline cadence (8 live sales calls, 12 JTBD switch interviews, 4 win-back calls):

The first is buyers who say "no decision." Verbatim from the most recent loss interview: "we just didn't think the problem was big enough yet." The second is buyers who lost to a comparable product after a feature-by-feature evaluation. The mix matters: we read the conversation log before we propose the plan, because the right plan for "no-decision" loss is different from the plan for "feature-comparison" loss.

The job is the unit. Each switch interview maps to a JTBD form: "Help me [verb] [object] when [situation]." For these accounts the dominant job is "Help me coordinate cross-functional output when the team has tripled and Slack threads outpace context." Forces of progress (push from the old reality, pull from the new), forces of resistance (anxiety about losing existing work, habit of the current tool), all logged per interview. Tom Klement's framing for the switch interview surfaces the buyer mental model the feature framework misses; we hire the product to make a job progress.

We open the diagnostic with rapport before research. Tactical empathy on the call, mirror their language, label the resistance, then surface the actual switching cost. The accusation audit clears emotional debris that direct questioning misses. The verbatim language goes into the substrate, not a paraphrased summary.

The customer language is the source of the message. We run TF-IDF on noun-phrase frequency across the call corpus, surface words they use (not the marketer paraphrase the brief team would have written from the homepage), and feed the verbatim into messaging-matrix. Andy Crestodina's rule: message-mining the call log beats focus-grouping the team's hypotheses. Joanna Wiebe's rule: the customer's words convert; the marketer's words explain. Both rules push the same direction.

We're going to bedrock here, past "the team thinks the website needs a refresh" to the law of physics underneath: which buyer state is actually leaking, and at what cost. The frontline conversation is the substrate; the office is downstream.

## Three divergent paths

Path 1 — narrative-first. If the diagnosis returns ">40% no-decision," reframe the homepage and pitch deck around the world-shift first.

Path 2 — competitive-first. If the diagnosis returns "majority feature comparison loss," double down on battle cards and displacement copy.

Path 3 — pricing-first. If the diagnosis returns "buyers stalled at quote stage," investigate willingness-to-pay before re-litigating the message.

## Plan

We will publish the chosen path inside 14 days, after the diagnosis lands. The diagnosis is the deliverable; the plan is the artifact that descends from it.
